Does a Commonly-Implemented Court Diversion Strategy Improve Attendance in Elementary School?
Evaluation of the Ramsey County Family Truancy Intervention Program
with Clea McNeely, Janet Rosenbaum, Lynette Renner, and Besu Alemu

Motivation and Relevance

  • Chrnoic absenteeism is widespread (USED, 2016)
    • Over 7 million students missed 15 or more days of school in 2015-16
    • Hidden educational crisis
  • Increasing demand for evidence-based interventions.
  • Increasing demand for the rigorous evaluation of the truancy programs in the literature (Fantuzzo et al., 2005; Stephen et al., 2010)
  • Most common type of truancy intervention program: Court-diversion programs (McNeely & Carpenter, 2018)

Introduction:

  • Chronic absenteeism is defined as:
    • Missing 15 or more school days in a school year
    • 2.4 million elementary students are chronically absent (DOE 2016)
  • Chronic Absenteeism is correlated with:
    • For individuals:
      • Lower academic performance (kobrin, 2009; Maynard et al., 2012)
      • Anti-social behaivior (Claes et al., 2009; Kearney, 2008a)
      • Substance abuse (Hirschfield & Gasper, 2011; Vaughn et al.,2013)
      • Negative labor market outcomes (Halfors et al., 2002; Stuit & Springer., 2010)
    • For Community:
      • Lower tax revenue (Tyler & Lofstrom, 2009; Stuit & Springer, 2010)
      • Higher crime rates (Tyler & Lofstrom, 2009; Stuit & Springer, 2010)

Previous & Current efforts to reduce absenteeism

  • Treatment Vs. Punishment
    • The pre-2000s correctional model
      • Direct referral to court
    • Court + Community-based service model:
      • Based on better theretical grounds
    • But, little progress has been made (Jacob & Lovett, 2017; Maynard et al., 2017)
      • Between 2002 - 2014, truancy rate constant at 11%
  • Prior evaluations suffer from (Maynard et al., 2013; Sutphen et al., 2010):
    • Small sample size
    • Short-term follow-up
    • Sample attrition
    • Dissimilar comparison groups

This study

  • Evaluate the effectiveness of a large-scale chronic absenteeism intervention program:
    • Implemented in an urban county since the early 2000s
    • Focuses on referral between 2006-2009
    • Approximately 500 referral annually
    • Low-cost, but involves participation of parents, schools, community partners and legal strategy

Data

  • Department of Education & Department of Human Services:
    • Student-year level data (2004-2015)
      • Yearly attendance rate
      • Socio-demographic variables
      • Standardized test scores, math and reading
      • Child welfare involvement
  • Absenteeism data from school districts in program county:
    • Student-day level data (2004-2015)
    • Unexcused vs. excused absence information
  • Program data from county attorney's office:
    • Referral dates by stages

Description of the program

  • The intervention program consists of three steps:
    • Step 1. Referral to large-group parent meeting
      • After 5 or more unexcused absences
      • Explains the legal and social consequences of continuing chronic absenteeism
    • Step 2. Referral to small-group school attendance review team (SART)
      • After 5 additional unexcused absences
      • Small group creates constructive formalized plan to improve attendance
    • Step 3. Petition to family court/referral to child welfare
      • Court-ordered to comply with a case plan developed by child protection office

Identification strategy

  • Primary concern of program evaluation studies: Problem of selection
  1. Matching on pre-treatment characteristics:
    • Comparison pool come from adjecant County that did not have the county-wide absenteeism program
    • Matching process ensures that the comparison group is characteristically similar to the treatment group
  2. Difference-in-differences method:
    • Leverages panel data, further eliminates time invariant unobserved characteristics that might have affected the selection process

Difference-in-differences


We estimate following generalized DD basic model:

\begin{align*} y_{it} = \alpha +\beta P_{it} + X_{it}\gamma + \rho_{t} + \gamma_{i} + \varepsilon_{it} \end{align*}


  • $P_{it}$ is a binary indicator for pre- and post-program referral for student i where we set t=0 (a year of the referral) as the reference category
  • $X_{it}$ are the vector of time-varying student characteristics
  • $\rho_{t}$ is grade dummies that captures time-trends
  • $\gamma_{i}$ controls for the time-constant unobserved individual level characteristics
  • $\varepsilon_{it}$ is an error term assumed to be i.i.d.

Conclusion

  • Failed to find evidence that the program has a statistically positive effect on attendance.
    • No effect in the long-term
    • No effect in the short-term
  • Regression to the mean/transitory nature of absence
    • Emphasizes importance of comparison group
    • Bias can be larger in studies that examines short-term effect of the program

Limitation

  • Possible heterogeneous school-level interventions
  • Does not account for long-term effect on the unexcuse absence

Policy implication

based on the findings and the literature

  1. Need Regular evaluation of programs to identify best practices
    • Quality of implementation seems critical
    • Implementation is particularly challenging:
      • Parental substance abuse
      • School bullying
      • Transportation challenges
    • Collect data on program implementaion, fidelity
  2. Multi-dimensional interventions (Epstein, 2001)
    • Greater school-based efforts:
      • School districts and school need more local funding to tackle the problem they are facing
        • Hiring more social workers or attendance staff
        • school-based health and mental health services
      • more implentation of strategies that involve families and the community, have a restorative focus
      • School districts and school need more local funding
