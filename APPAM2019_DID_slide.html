Motivation and relevance

  • ECE subsidies can increase human capital accumulation, especially for children from disadvantaged families.(Barnett, 2008; Chaparro & Sojourner, 2015; Duncan & Magnuson, 2013; Duncan & Sojourner, 2013; Heckman, 2006)
  • Many policymakers want to invest in ECE:
    • Uncertainty remains about how subsidy expansions affect crowd-out, prices, and other outcomes.
    • Less study of how ECE subsidy policies affect the ECE services market
  • Large variation in ECE subsidies in Minnesota in recent years
This study aims to quantify how early care and education (ECE) subsidies affect the supply and price of care services, with particular attention to the heterogeneity in the effect depending on alternative program design features.

It is well known that ECE subsidies can increase human capital accumulation, especially for children from disadvantaged families.

Many policymakers want to invest in ECE but vast uncertainty remains about how subsidy expansions would affect crowd-out, prices, and other outcomes.

There has been much less study of how ECE subsidy policies affect the ECE services market, which can help economists and policymakers predict trade-offs in subsidy design.

Improving ECE subsidy policy at scale requires understanding how ECE providers will respond to alternative subsidy policy designs. For example, in-kind public provision may crowd out private and nonprofit providers while subsidies via vouchers may have different effects. We study how funding changes via diﬀerent types of programs within diﬀerent Minnesota communities over time relate to changes in local ECE supply. We are in a good position to study this as the Early care and education subsidies in Minnesota almost doubled between 2013 and 2017 from approximately $200 million to $400 million

Research Question

  • To what extent does each additional subsidy of $10,000 per child in a community's population affect:
    • the quantity and types of care services
    • market prices of care services
    • differently if the subsidy target infants and toddlers (age 0-2) or preschoolers (age 3-5)
    • differently if the subsidy target public providers or private ones
This study asks the question "To what extent does each additional subsidy of $10,000 per child to the local area affects various outcomes such as quantity and price of different types of care services. How are these impacts different if the subsidy target infants and toddlers (age 0-2)? If they are restricted to be used only by public providers or private ones?

There has been a number of previous studies that looks at the effect of subsidies on the changes in the quantity in the market. It has been found that the Funding for child care subsidies have been associated with growth in the number of providers. Bassok, Fitzpatrick, Loeb (2014) studied effects of universal prekindergarten introductions in Georgia (voucher) and Oklahoma (school based) on ECE capacity, ECE workforce and pay. Bassok, Miller Galdo (2016) studied the effect of Floridas rollout on county-level total capacity. They found some evidence of crowdout of private capacity using synthetic control and difference-in-difference methods. Neither could look at effects on prices or quality, key outcomes for understanding the costs and impacts of policy.

Our primary analysis will focus on locales defined along school district boundaries because this will facilitate eventual connection to child outcomes (special-education share or student achievement at the school district by year by cohort level). Measures

  • Outcome
    • Total quantity
      • (by provider type (Center, Family, and Public), by age group (infant, toddler, and preschool))
    • Average Price
      • (by provider type (Center, Family, and Public), by age group (infant, toddler, and preschool))
    • Share highly-rated under the state's quality rating and information system (3 or 4 stars in Parent Aware QRIS system)
  • Treatment
    • Policy dollar amounts
For the outcomes, we use total quanity and average price defined at the SD, due to the richness of the data, we are able to decompose these outcome measures by the provider type, and by age group.

Another key outcome measure is the share highly-rated in the SD under the state's quality rating system, we coded a provider highly rated if they were ever rated above 3 or 4 stars in Parent Aware QRIS system.

Methods


Difference-in-differences model:

\begin{align*} y_{it} = \beta P_{it} + X_{it}\gamma + \gamma_{i} + \rho_{t} + \varepsilon_{it} \end{align*}


  • $y_{it}$ represents an outcome: local slots per child, average price among local providers, share highly-rated among local providers either overall or for different provider-type subsamples and different age groups.
  • $P_{it}$ represents the policy dollar amount of interest, ten thousands of dollars of local ECE subsidy funding per local child aggregating across relevant ECE programs for the locale-year.
  • $X_{it}$ is a control variable for locale-year all-day kindergarten funding.
  • $\gamma_{i}$ controls for the time-constant unobserved individual level characteristics
  • $\rho_{t}$ is a set of year dummies that remove the influence of average year-to-year differences shared across all Minnesota locales.
  • $\varepsilon_{it}$ measure locale-year specific unobservable influence.
This is the difference-in-difference model, we used for the estimation.
Ideally, communities would be randomly assigned different levels of Pit. Instead, we have some communities getting sudden increases in particular funding streams while others do not.

Results

Estimated effects of overall funding and sector-specific funding on capacity

We consider these results preliminary as we await better policy data and more years of outcome data. We'd especially appreciate feedback on struc- ture of the analysis. We would advise against taking the current estimates seriously. That said, we will interpret the results here just to give a flavor for the kinds of interpretations we are hoping to inform.

The estimates suggest that an additional $10,000 in total funding for childcare subsidies increases in a locale and year raise increases childcare capacity provided there by an average of 4.3 slots with a 95% confidence interval ranging from 1.1 to 7.1 (Table 3:Column 1). The low end of this range seems quite consistent with average childcare costs. The high-end could be plausible if subsidies also drew in additional private resources, from parent co-pays and the like. This effect combines a +2.8 slot effect on private providers, almost exclusively in centers, and a +1.5 slot effect on public providers (Columns 2-5).

Preliminary Conclusion

  • Heterogenious effect of funding on quantity, although not always in a way anticipated:
    • Overall funding disproportionately increase center-based capacity
    • Private funding disproportionately increase center-based capacity
    • Public funding disproportionately increase center-based capacity
  • Heterogenious effect of funding on price, although not always in a way anticipated:
    • Overall funding disproportionately decrease center-based price
    • Private funding disproportionately decrease center-based price
    • Public funding appears to have not statistically significant effect on price
