Problems with existing measures of parental preference

  • The aggregate measures (across sex of child) overlook important family-level heterogeniety in preferences:
    • In U.S., where the sex-ratio is 1.05, the biologically normal sex-ratio without any interference, there exists clear preferences for the sex of children.
      • 2000 and 2003 Gallop Poll survey asks "Suppose you could only have one child. Would you prefer that it be a boy or girl?"
        • Men: 19% prefer daughter, 48% prefer son
        • Women: 35% prefer daughter, 30% prefer son
Problem with these measures of parental preferences is that they ignores important family-level heterogeneity in preference, for example, in U.S, sex-ratio is 1.05,

biologically normal sex-ratio and based on this measure, one can concludes that parents in U.S. is gender-neutral, whereas in fact, according to the Gallop poll survey, 19% of men prefer to have daughter and 48% prefer to have son.

35% women prefers daughter and 30% prefers son.

studies that rely on the aggregate fertility data requires the strong assumption of homogeneous preferences within the area of interest.


Problems with existing measures of parental preference - Continued

  • E[I|B, Group = 1] = 0.5; E[I|G, Group = 1] = 0.5, E[I|B, Group = 2] = 0.5, E[I|G, Group = 2] = 0.5.
  • while true, misses important and interesting heterogeneity in parental preference:
  • E[I|B, P = B, Group = 1] = 1; E[I|B, P = G, Group = 1] = 0.
The problem is illustrated in this simple matrix, group 1 has strong preference for sex of their child and allocates parental input accordingly.

If the sex of child is what they expect, they allocate input of 1, and zero otherwise.

In group 2, they are gender-neutral and allocates same input level regardless of the sex of their child. The problem is that expectation of input conditional on sex of child will be at 0.5 for both group 1 and group 2.

And size of the effect of parental preferences for group 1 has been attenuated.

Chapter 2: Effect of single-sex schools on student outcomes: Evidence from Seoul, South Korea
In the 2nd chapter, I examine the effect of single-sex schools on student outcomes using data from city of Seoul, South Korea.

Introduction

  • The pros and cons of single-sex (SS) schooling have been a long debated topic
    • No clear consensus in the effects of SS on academic outcomes:
      • Meta-analysis finds larger portion of studies finds positive effect (Mael et al., 2005)
      • Critics argue, positive effect of SS on academic outcomes (Halpern et al., 2011):
        • Weak scientific evidence
        • Negative consequences (gender stereotyping and sexism)
      • Threats to causal inference
        • Non-random selection (Mael et al., 2005; Halpern et al., 2011)
        • Potential systematic difference between SS and Co-educational schools (Teacher, Curriculum and Budget)
The pros and cons of SS schooling has been long debated topic internationally.

There is no clear consensus in the effect of SS schooling on academic outcomes, but studies that review the literature suggests marority of studies find positive effect.

Critics argue that positive effect of SS schooling on academic outcomes are grounded in weak scientific evidence and SS schools can engender negative consequences such as gender stereotyping and sexism.

Threats to causal inference in the SS school literature, especially in the U.S. context has been the non-random selection of students to the SS schools and systematic difference between SS and coeducational schools.

Equalization policy in Seoul, South Korea

  • Randomly assigns middle and high school students within school-districts of their residence
  • Implemented since the 1970s
  • Designed to provide a homogeneous educational environment
    • Prohibit schools from selecting students based on academic performance or SES
  • Compliance is high
    • Applies to all middle and high school students in Seoul
    • If don't like the assigned school, whole family has to re-locate to other school districts, where they are again randomly assigned to schools within school district
To address the non-random selection problem in the literature, a number of studies have used equalization policy of South Korea, which randomly assigns middle and high-school students within school-districts of their residence.

The policy has been implemented since 1970s

Designed to provide a homogeneous educational environment across the country

And prohibit schools from selecting students based on academic performance or socio-economic status

Compliance to the policy is high. Applies to all middle and high school students in Seoul.

Identification strategy

  • Rely on the Equalization policy (Random assignment policy, Check credibility)
  • Matching for any imbalance (residential or other unobserved sorting is potentially possible)
  • Difference-in-differences estimator
    • to eliminate further unobserved heterogeneity that might be present

      • \begin{align*} y_{it} = \alpha +\beta SS_{it} + X_{it}\delta + \tau_{t}+ \gamma_{i} + \epsilon_{it} \end{align*}


      • $Y_{it}$ is the school outcomes for student i at time t.
      • $SS_{it}$ is a binary time indicator for pre- and post-school type transition from elementary school to middle SS school for student i.
      • $X_{it}$ is the vector of time-varying student characteristics
      • $\tau_{t}$ is time dummies that captures time-trends
      • $\gamma_{i}$ controls for the time-constant unobserved individual level characteristics
      • $\epsilon_{it}$ is an error term assumed to be i.i.d.
For identification strategy, I rely on the equalization policy, the random assignment policy,

I test the credibility of this assumption by checking pre-treatment balance using the panel data.

I also conduct matching and difference-in-difference methods to eliminate further unobserved heterogeneity that might be present.

This study

  • Evaluate the effectiveness of a large-scale chronic absenteeism intervention program that:
    • Most common type of truancy intervention in elementary school, court-diversion program
    • Implemented in the Ramsey county since the early 2000s
    • Approximately 500 elementary students are referred to the program annually
    • Relatively low-cost, but involves participation of parents, schools, community partners and legal strategy
  • First large-scale evaluation of absenteeism intervention program using population-level administrative data
This study examines causal effect of the large scale absence intervention program , that is most common type of truancy intervention in elementary school, the court-diversion program.
Has been implemented in the Ramsey county for almost 20 years.

Approximately 500 elementary students who are referred to the program annually.
The program is relatively low-cost, but involves participation of multiple parties. this study is especially relevent and timely, as the court-diversion type program that we are evaluating is widely used across the school districts in U.S., about 60% are implementing the court-diversion programs, and recently, state of Texas and Tennessee mandated that all school districts implement court-diversion programs before petitioning to court for truancy case.

For the evaluation of the program, We use 12 years of student-year level data from multiple state agencies including department of education, department of human services, the unique feature of this dataset is that the multiple state agency data is linked across individuals over time, so it gives relatively rich information about the individual’s attendance history, academic performance as well as family background.
We also use daily absentieesm data from five school-districts in the program county that distinquished unexcused from excused absences. Since we only have this data for the program county, we use this data to examine the short-term monthly-level effect of the program within an academic-year as a robustness check of the main results.
And finally, we use program data from county attorney's office with information on referral dates.

Description of the program

  • The intervention program consists of three steps:
    • Step 1. Referral to large-group parent meeting
      • After 5 or more unexcused absences
      • Explains the legal and social consequences of continuing chronic absenteeism
    • Step 2. Referral to small-group school attendance review team (SART)
      • After 5 additional unexcused absences
      • Small group creates constructive formalized plan to improve attendance
    • Step 3. Referral to child welfare/petition to family court
      • Court-ordered to comply with a case plan developed by child protection office
The program consists of three-steps.

first step is the large group parent meeting after a student makes 5 or more unexcused absences, at the parent meeting, county attorney provide information about the negative consequences of the continued school absences, information about the program and eventual legal consequences.

After the first meeting, students' attendances are closely monitored by the school and those who makes additional 5 or more unexcused absnneces are referred to 2nd step of the program, where students and parents meet with county attorneys and community counselors where they try to find more detail cause of the continued truancy and form a written contract to improve the attendance.

If the unexcused abnseces continue after the 2nd step, the final step of the program involves referral to the child protection office or family-court, where parents may be court-ordered to comply with a case plan developed by court.

Identification strategy

  • Primary concern of program evaluation studies: Problem of selection
  1. Matching on pre-treatment characteristics:
    • Comparison pool come from adjacent county that did not have the county-wide absenteeism program
    • Matching process ensures that the comparison group is characteristically similar to the treatment group on observed covariates
  2. Difference-in-differences method:
    • Leverages panel data, further eliminates time invariant unobserved characteristics that might have affected the selection process

    For identification strategy, we construct comparison group through matching on pre-treatment individual-level characteristics. The comparison pool come from the adjacent county that did not have the similarly structured program, but faces very similar macro and demographic trends.
    The matching process ensures that the comparison group is characteristically quite similar to the treatment on all the observables.

    After mathcing, there still remains a room for selection on unobservables, such as student motivation and behavior, or parental support, we use difference-in-difference method that leverages the panel data which difference out the time invariant unobservables in the error term.

Program and comparison county intervention conditions

This table compares the intervention condition between the Ramsey and the comparison county.
The key difference between the program and the comparison county was the systematic intervention by the county attorney’s office after five unexcused absences. In the comparison county, school personnel reported educational neglect directly to the county’s department of human services after seven or more unexcused absences.

The department of human services investigated the case, and made a determination as to whether to close the case, offer the parents voluntary case management, or petition the parent(s) to court.

In the comparison county, educational neglect was unlikely to be reported until it had escalated to a substantial number of days because school staff did not want to involve child protection services unless they deemed the situation to be very serious.

Difference-in-differences


We estimate the following DD model:

\begin{align*} y_{it} = \alpha +\beta P_{it} + X_{it}\gamma + \rho_{t} + \gamma_{i} + \varepsilon_{it} \end{align*}


  • $P_{it}$ is a binary indicator for pre- and post-program referral for student i where we set t=0 (a year of the referral) as the reference category
  • $X_{it}$ is the vector of time-varying student characteristics
  • $\rho_{t}$ is grade dummies that captures time-trends
  • $\gamma_{i}$ controls for the time-constant unobserved individual level characteristics
  • $\varepsilon_{it}$ is an error term assumed to be i.i.d.
This is the difference-in-difference model, we used for the estimation. beta is the parameter of interest and p is the binary indicator for pre-, post-program referral for student i. we set the year of referral as reference category.
We limit the anlaysis window to 2 years prior and 4 years after the referral.
Using difference in difference model, you can identify the program effect by differencing the changes in attendance before and after the intervention for each groups.

Robustness check

  • Concern of the main analysis
    • Attendance rate doesn't distinguish between excused/unexcused absence
    • Matching on attendance rate in the year of referral may contain a partial treatment effect
  • For the robustness check
    • Estimated short-term program effects using daily attendance data
Tne concerns about the main analysis is that
yearly attendance rate we used for the anlaysis aggregates excused/unexcused absences, so if the referred students had disproportionately higher unexcused absneces relative to the comparison group, our estimator could be biased downward.

Also, The fact that we are using the attendance rate in the year of referral as a matching variable may be problematic because it contain a partial treatment effect.

To address this concern, we use daily attendance data from program county, the advantage of using the daily attendance data is that we have data on excused and unexcused absences, so this allows us to conduct matching based on unexucsed abseces.

Also, we are able to conduct matching using the pre-referral monthly absence data.

Conclusion

  • Failed to find evidence that the program has a statistically positive effect on attendance.
    • No effect in the long-term
    • No effect in the short-term
  • Regression to the mean/transitory nature of absence
    • Emphasizes importance of comparison group
    • Bias can be larger in studies that examine short-term effects of the program
To summarize the study, The results of our study shows that the program did not have a positive effect on improving the attendance of the referred students, either in the short-term or up to four years after program referral.

We observe some degree of "regression to the mean" or transitory nature of absence, suggesting absence is often clustered at certain points in time within the academic year. we argue that this is the important issue to be addressed in any types of evaluation study of absentieesm intervention program.


The magnitude of bias may be larger when the follow-up period is limited to short period of time, especially within a school year.
