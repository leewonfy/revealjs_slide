Introduction

  • The mertis of single-sex (SS) schooling have been a long debated topic
    • No clear consensus in the effects of SS on academic outcomes:
      • Meta-analysis finds larger portion of studies finds positive effect (Mael et al., 2005)
      • Recent quasi-experimental studies find positive effect (Lee et al., 2014; Jackson 2012, 2019; Park, Behrman and Choi, 2013)
      • Critics argue, positive effect of SS on academic outcomes (Halpern et al., 2011):
        • Weak scientific evidence
        • Negative consequences (gender stereotyping and sexism)
    • Threats to causal inference
      • Non-random selection (Mael et al., 2005; Halpern et al., 2011)
      • Potential systematic difference between SS and Co-educational schools (Teacher, Curriculum and Budget)
To give you some background, the merits of SS schooling has been widely discussed, and there are many studies that examines the effect of SS schooling on academic outcomes.

There are no clear consensus regarding the direction or size of the effect, but studies generally find positive effect on student's academic outcomes.

Especially, recent quasi-experimental studies also suggest positive effects on the academic outcomes, size of the effects varies widely, but it ranging up to 0.05 to 0.2 SD increaase in the test scores.
Critics argue that positive effect of SS schooling on academic outcomes are grounded in weak scientific evidence and SS schools can engender negative consequences such as gender stereotyping and sexism.

Threats to causal inference in the SS school literature, especially in the U.S. context has been the non-random selection of students to the SS schools and systematic difference between SS and coeducational schools.

Equalization policy in Seoul, South Korea

  • Randomly assigns middle and high school students within school-districts of their residence
  • Implemented since the 1970s
  • Designed to provide a homogeneous educational environment
    • Prohibit schools from selecting students based on academic performance or SES
    • Reduce excessive competition for middle school entrance exam
  • Compliance is high
    • Applies to all middle and high school students in Seoul
    • If don't like the assigned school, whole family has to re-locate to other school districts, where they are again randomly assigned to schools within school district
To address the non-random selection problem in the literature, many studies have used equalization policy of South Korea, which randomly assigns middle and high-school students within school-districts of their residence.

The policy has been implemented since 1970s in response to the growing pressure on middle school entrance exam in the 1960s

Designed to provide a homogeneous educational environment across the country.

And prohibit schools from selecting students based on academic performance or socio-economic status

Compliance to the policy is high. Applies to all middle and high school students in Seoul.

This study

  • Examine the effect of single-sex school on students' academic outcomes among middle school students
  • Examine the effect of single-sex school on students' non-academic outcomes among middle school students
  • This is the first study that uses within-student variation to identify causal effect of SS school
  • Aims to find evidence on the underlying mechanism using rich survey data
The purpose of this study is to examine effect of SS schooling on student outcomes in middle school and explore possible mechanisms of the effect using student-level panel data.


Data

  • Seoul Education Longitudinal Study (SELS):
    • Annually-surveyed
    • Student-level panel data
    • Students in City of Seoul
    • One cohort data (4th grade in 2010..9th grade in 2016)
    • Samples about 5,000 students
I use annually surveyed student-level panel data from city of Seoul.
I use one cohort data in this study who are in 4th grade in 2010 and becomes 9th graders in 2016. Sampled 5,000 students from City of Seoul.


Identification strategy

  • Rely on the Equalization policy (Random assignment policy, Check credibility)
  • Matching for any imbalance (residential or other unobserved sorting is potentially possible)
  • Difference-in-differences estimator
    • to eliminate further unobserved heterogeneity that might be present

      • \begin{align*} y_{it} = \alpha +\beta SS_{it} + X_{it}\delta + \tau_{t}+ \gamma_{i} + \epsilon_{it} \end{align*}


      • $Y_{it}$ is the school outcomes for student i at time t.
      • $SS_{it}$ is a binary time indicator for pre- and post-school type transition from elementary school to middle SS school for student i.
      • $X_{it}$ is the vector of time-varying student characteristics
      • $\tau_{t}$ is time dummies that captures time-trends
      • $\gamma_{i}$ controls for the time-constant unobserved individual level characteristics
      • $\epsilon_{it}$ is an error term assumed to be i.i.d.
For identification strategy, I rely on the equalization policy, the random assignment policy,

I test the credibility of this assumption by checking pre-treatment balance using the panel data.

I also conduct matching and difference-in-difference methods to eliminate further unobserved heterogeneity that might be present.

Conclusion 1/2

  • This study examines effect of SS schooling in Middle school on student outcomes using student-level panel data from Seoul, South Korea
  • This is the first study that uses within-student variation in the academic achievement to identify causal effect of SS school
  • Preliminary results suggest:
    • SS schooling have positive effect for girls while smaller and statistically insignificant effect was found for boys
      • Girls: +0.13 s.d.(Math), +0.23 s.d.(Korean), null (English), +0.157 s.d.(Overall)
      • Boys: null s.d.(Math), null s.d.(Korean), null (English), null s.d.(Overall)
    • Similar findings in the peer effects literature: positive effect of increasing share of female students on academic outcomes (Eren, 2017; Whitmore, 2005; Lu and Anderson, 2015)
To summarize, This study examines effect of SS schooling in Middle school on students outcomes using student-level panel data from Seoul.

This is the first study that uses within-student variation in the academic achievement to identify causal effect of SS school

The results suggest that SS schooling have positive effect for girls while null effect was found for boys.

This finding is consistent with studies that are based on RCT in the peer effects literature that generally finds positive effect of increasing share of female students on academic outcomes and no effect for male students.

About 13 percent of schools in Korea are private, most at the secondary school level, private schools receive public funding. In return, they dont charge school fees for compulsory education and cannot charge higher fees for upper secondary than those public schools charge. Private schools adhere to the national curriculum and teachers are required to meet the same standards for teacher certification. The key difference is that teachers are employed directly by the school rather than by the Ministry which means that they are not required to rotate among schools, as are public school teachers. School funding is very centrailzed, with the Ministry of Education providing more than 75 percent of school funding.
