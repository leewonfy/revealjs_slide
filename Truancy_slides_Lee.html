Schools have moved away from correctional models and now the most common type of intervention for chronic absenteeism is court diversion programs that combine

Court diversion models are more theoretically sound than correctional models because they emphasize the importance of tackling multiple levels of students environment to improve school engagement.

This study

    • Implemented in an urban county since the early 2000s

This study examines effectiveness of the large scale absenteeism intervention program targeted to elementary school students. which has been implemented in the county since early 2000s, approximately 500 elementary students were referred to the program annually.
In this study, we focuses on students referred between 2006-2009, due mainly to the data agreement with school districts.
The program is relatively low-cost, but involves participation of parents, schools and community partners and eventually, petition to family court.

Description of the program

  • The intervention program consists of three steps:
    • Step 1. Referral to large-group parent meeting
      • After 5 or more unexcused absences
      • Explains the legal and social consequences of continuing chronic absenteeism
    • Step 2. Referral to small-group school attendance review team (SART)
      • After 5 additional unexcused absences
      • Small group creates constructive formalized plan to improve attendance
    • Step 3. Referral to child welfare/petition to family court
      • Court-ordered to comply with a case plan developed by child protection office
The program consists of three-steps.

[Click] first step is the large group parent meeting after a student makes 5 or more unexcused absences, at the parent meeting, county attorney provide information about the negative consequences of the continued school absences, information about the program and eventual legal consequences.

[Click] After the first meeting, students' attendances are closely monitored by the school and those who makes additional 5 or more unexcused absnneces are referred to 2nd step of the program, where students and parents meet with county attorneys and community counselors where they try to find more detail cause of the continued truancy and form a written contract to improve the attendance.

[Click] If the unexcused abnseces continue after the 2nd step, the final step of the program involves referral to the child protection office or family-court, where parents may be court-ordered to comply with a case plan developed by court.

Identification strategy

  • Primary concern of program evaluation studies: Problem of selection
  1. Matching on pre-treatment characteristics:
    • Comparison pool come from adjacent county that did not have the county-wide absenteeism program
    • Matching process ensures that the comparison group is characteristically similar to the treatment group on observed covariates
  2. Difference-in-differences method:
    • Leverages panel data, further eliminates time invariant unobserved characteristics that might have affected the selection process
    [Click]The primary concern in program evaluation studies based on observational data is the problem of selection, that characteristics of referred students are likely to differ systematically from non-referred students in ways that affect future trajectories of attendance.
    In order to mitigate these selection problem, we employ two strategies:

    [Click]First, we construct comparison group through matching on pre-treatment individual-level characteristics. The comparison pool come from the adjacent county that did not have the similarly structured program, but faces very similar macro and demographic trends.
    The matching process ensures that the comparison group is characteristically quite similar to the treatment on all the observables.

    [Click]But, after mathcing, there still remains a room for selection on unobservables, such as student motivation and behavior, or parental support, so, in order to mitigate the selection, we use difference-in-difference method that leverages the panel data which difference out the time invariant unobservables in the error term.

Difference-in-differences


We estimate the following DD model:

\begin{align*} y_{it} = \alpha +\beta P_{it} + X_{it}\gamma + \rho_{t} + \gamma_{i} + \varepsilon_{it} \end{align*}


  • $P_{it}$ is a binary indicator for pre- and post-program referral for student i where we set t=0 (a year of the referral) as the reference category
  • $X_{it}$ is the vector of time-varying student characteristics
  • $\rho_{t}$ is grade dummies that captures time-trends
  • $\gamma_{i}$ controls for the time-constant unobserved individual level characteristics
  • $\varepsilon_{it}$ is an error term assumed to be i.i.d.
This is the difference-in-difference model, we used for the estimation. beta is the parameter of interest and p is the binary indicator for pre-, post-program referral for student i. we set the year of referral as reference category.
We limit the anlaysis window to 2 years prior and 4 years after the referral.
Using difference in difference model, under the parallel assumptions, you can identify the program effect by differencing the changes in attendance before and after the intervention for each groups.
Parallel trend assumption basically requires that in the absence of treatment, the difference in attendance rate between the 'treatment' and 'comparison' group is constant over time.

Conclusion

  • Failed to find evidence that the program has a statistically positive effect on attendance.
    • No effect in the long-term
    • No effect in the short-term
  • Regression to the mean/transitory nature of absence
    • Emphasizes importance of comparison group
    • Bias can be larger in studies that examine short-term effects of the program
[Click]So, to summarize the study, The results of our study shows that the program did not have a positive effect on improving the attendance of the referred students, either in the short-term or up to four years after program referral.

[Click]We observe some degree of "regression to the mean" or transitory nature of absence, suggesting absence is often clustered at certain points in time within the academic year. we argue that this is the important issue to be addressed in any types of evaluation study of absentieesm intervention program.
Because this can be the systematic cause of bias in the field when counterfactual is not carefully constructed.
The magnitude of bias may be larger when the follow-up period is limited to short period of time, especially within a school year.

Limitation

  • No measures of implementation
  • Does not account for long-term effect on the unexcuse absences
Some limitations of the study. The biggest limitation of this study is that there is no good measure of implementation of the program, which make it difficult to disentangle whether the null findings are due to program contents, implementation fidelity, or both.

Second, for the main analysis of the study, we used yearly attendance data that aggregates unexcused and excused absences, so we could not evaluate whether the program affected the long-term trajectory of unexcused absences, which this program really targets.

  • Need rigorous evaluations of programs to identify best practices

    • Strive to collect data on program implementaion, fidelity

      So, a couple of policy implications drawn from the findings of this study and the based on the review of the literature.
      First, we would argue that there needs to be more critical evaluation of existing policy across the nation, to ensure that current practices are truly of benefit to the students and schools who are the recipients of those procedures.
      The evidence suggests that a variety of different types of programs can be successful. But, As with all programs, the quality of implementation seems critical.
      Working with school districts to find systematic ways to track implementation fidelity, and collect information from parents’ and students’ about their experiences with the truancy diversion program would be beneficial for future evaluation purposes.
      We noticed the large proportion of the managemnet work was conducted manually including coding of absences and program referral, this adds burden of extra paperworks to the already busy school administrators, We suggest implementation of online-based application tools that allows administrators to streamline management of the program,
      real-time activity monitoring and reporting enables to keep the quality of implementation up to certain standards, and timely interventions., this is already widely used strategy in many school programs and have shown to be effective.

Policy implication - II

based on the findings and the literature

Quote from a superintendent of a school district in the program county:

"It's all about relationships. We need ways to better engage with these kids."
  • Bring in the interpersonal component to the existing program:
      • Maximize personal contact and opportunities to build trusting relationships
      • Mentor-mentee (program staff, teacher, police officers)
      • Behavioral Economics - Nudging (Rogers & Feller, 2017)
Additionally, I think it is important to design the intervention to maximize the personal contact and relationships.

Few programs that are shown to be highly-effective in improving the school engagement has the common feature of some type of mentor-mentee relationship with the children and family. so, there is usually one individual who is responsible for ensuring multiple students connects with school, the mentor person can be a parent, program staff, teacher or even police officers.

Right now, the court diversion programs consists of, basically as a set of services lacking the relationship component. The relationship-intensive program is expensive and difficult to scale, but I think it is important to think about a cost-effective way to bring in this connection in to the diversion models.

there is a emerging literature in behavioral economics that sending series of encouragement text messages to parents or guardians affects child attendance in a postive way. This type of intervention could be an cost-effective direction that schools can test using experimental design.
