Motivation and relevance

  • ECE subsidies can increase human capital accumulation, especially for children from disadvantaged families.(Barnett, 2008; Chaparro & Sojourner, 2015; Duncan & Magnuson, 2013; Duncan & Sojourner, 2013; Heckman, 2006)
  • Many policymakers want to invest in ECE:
    • Uncertainty remains about how subsidy expansions affect crowd-out, prices, and other outcomes.
    • Less study of how ECE subsidy policies affect the ECE services market
  • Large variation in ECE subsidies in Minnesota in recent years

Research Question

  • To what extent does each additional subsidy of $10,000 per child in a community's population affect:
    • the quantity and types of care services
    • market prices of care services
    • differently if the subsidy target infants and toddlers (age 0-2) or preschoolers (age 3-5)
    • differently if the subsidy target public providers or private ones

Methods


Difference-in-differences model:

\begin{align*} y_{lt} = \beta P_{lt} + X_{lt}\gamma + \gamma_{l} + \rho_{t} + \varepsilon_{lt} \end{align*}


  • $y_{lt}$ represents an quanity and price outcomes
  • $P_{lt}$ represents the policy dollar amount of interest, ten thousands of dollars per local child
  • $X_{lt}$ is a control variable for locale-year all-day kindergarten funding.
  • $\gamma_{l}$ is SD fixed effects
  • $\rho_{t}$ is year fixed effects locales.
  • $\varepsilon_{lt}$ measure locale-year specific unobservable influence.

Results

Estimated effects of overall funding and sector-specific funding on capacity

We consider these results preliminary as we await better policy data and more years of outcome data. That said, we will interpret the results here just to give a flavor for the kinds of interpretations we are hoping to inform.

If you look at the first column of the table, The estimates suggest that an additional $10,000 in total funding for childcare subsidies increases total capacity by an average of 4.3 slots with a 95% confidence interval ranging from 1.1 to 7.1. The low end of this range seems quite consistent with average childcare costs. The high-end could be plausible if subsidies also drew in additional private resources, from parent co-pays and the like. This effect combines a +2.8 slot effect on private providers, almost exclusively in centers, and a +1.5 slot effect on public providers (Columns 2-5).

All-Day-K funding also leads to increased capacity, about 1 slot per $10,000. All-Day-K funding seems to hurt family providers especially, perhaps by cutting into their \profitable" school-age care demand. However, it has positive +1.9 slot effect on center care, which is not so obvious why the effects would be so different. There's also a positive estimated effect on public capacity (not All-Day K though).

When total funding is split by sector, the private-providers targeted funding and on public-provider targeted funding, the effect of private-provider funding does have a large positive +3.4 slot per $10,000 effect on private capacity, especially at centers. This is sensible as private-provider funding should increase demand for private providers and, perhaps, additional subsidy opens up doors to centers for families that would otherwise not be able to afford them. Family providers may not be preferred by those with new options, as reflected in the small and statistically insignificant estimated 0.2 slot effect.

Funding for public providers is also estimated to raise private centers' capacity (1.6 slots/$10,000), which is less sensible. Further, funding for private providers is estimated to raise public capacity. That appears quite odd but could arise from our current misclassification of ELSP funding as private-provider only, when it is actually owing to both public and private providers. In contrast, funding for public providers does not appear to raise public capacity.
