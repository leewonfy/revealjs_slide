To give you some background, the advantages and disadvantages of SS schooling on student outcomes has been widely discussed, many studies have examined the effect of SS schooling on academic outcomes.

Yet, there are no clear consensus on its effects and its implication, Meta-analysis on the literature finds larger portion of studies finds positive effect, and recent quasi-experimental studies generally find positive effect on student's academic outcomes, with effect size ranges between 0.05 to 0.2 SD.


Critics argue that positive effect of SS schooling on academic outcomes are grounded in weak scientific evidence and SS schools may lead to negative consequences such as gender stereotyping and sexism.

One of the threats to causal inference in the SS education literature has been the non-random selection of students to the SS schools that cause systematic difference in student characteristics between SS and coeducational schools.

Equalization Policy in Seoul, South Korea

  • Randomly assigns middle and high school students within school-districts of their residence
  • Implemented since the 1970s
  • Designed to provide a homogeneous educational environment
    • Prohibit schools from selecting students based on academic performance or SES
    • Reduce excessive competition for middle school entrance exam
  • Compliance is high
    • Applies to all middle and high school students in Seoul
    • If don't like the assigned school, whole family has to re-locate to other school districts, where they are again randomly assigned to schools within school district
  • Does the data provide evidence to support that the policy is implemented randomly?
    • Previous studies lacks the evidence, due to use of cross-sectional (population) data (Park, Behrman and Choi (2013; Dustmann, Ku et al., 2018; Sohn, 2016; Lee et al., 2014).
To address the non-random selection problem, one of the common instruments that are widely used in Korean Context is the Equalization policy of South Korea., which is a lottery-based enrollment system that randomly assigns middle and high-school students within school-districts of their residence.

The policy has been implemented since 1970s to establish a uniform educational environment across the country and prevent schools from discriminating against students based on their previous academic performance or socio-economic status.

Designed to provide a homogeneous educational environment across the country.

Given that this policy applies to all middle school students in the city of Seoul, Compliance to the policy is high. The only way to transfer from undesired school assignment is to relocate to a different school district, where a student will once again subject to another random assignment among schools within the new district.
Many studies have used equalization policy of South Korea to examine the effect of SS education, but, the key weakness of the previous studies that rely on this policy instrument, is that most of these studies use cross-sectional data that are unable to test the pre-treatment balance, the baseline equivalence of those who enrolls to SS and coeducation schools.
One contribution of this study, is that this study address the issue by leveraging student-level panel data to see if the two groups are equal in expectations in observables prior to the assignment to each typs of schools.

Secondly, this paper examines the effect of SS education on non-academic outcomes. Data

  • Seoul Education Longitudinal Study (SELS):
    • Annually-surveyed
    • Six waves of survey data (4th grade in 2010..9th grade in 2016)
    • One cohort data
    • Samples about 3,500 students
    • Two-stage cluster random sampling method, randomly selected 289 schools in the city of Seoul and two classes within each selected school.
    • Student's educational environment, acheivement, and also collected from parents, teachers, and schools.
I use annually surveyed student-level panel data from city of Seoul.
I use one cohort data in this study who are in 4th graders in 2010 and becomes 9th graders in 2016. the survey sampled 3,500 students from City of Seoul. The data collection uses Two-stage cluster random sampling method, randomly selected 289 schools and two classes within each selected school.

The information collected by the survey pertains to multiple dimensions of students’ educational environment, and data are also collected from parents, teachers. The survey was conducted in July of each year, before the start of the summer vacation. The SELS conducted academic skill assessments for Korean, English, and math in each year of the survey. Each test lasted for 40 or 45 minutes per subject, and each test was comprised of 25-35 multiple-choice questions for elementary and middle school students, respectively, and designed to test the curriculum covered in class during the first half of the semester.

Identification Strategy

  • Matching on pre-treated observables (residential or other unobserved sorting is potentially possible)
  • Two-way Fixed Effects Model (Difference-in-differences estimator)
    • to eliminate further unobserved heterogeneity that might be present

      • \begin{align*} y_{it} = \alpha +\beta SS_{it} + X_{it}\delta + \tau_{t}+ \gamma_{i} + \epsilon_{it} \end{align*}


      • $Y_{it}$ is the school outcomes for student i at time t.
      • $SS_{it}$ is a binary time indicator for pre- and post-school type transition from elementary school to middle SS school for student i.
      • $X_{it}$ is the vector of time-varying student characteristics.
      • $\tau_{t}$ is time dummies that captures time-trends.
      • $\gamma_{i}$ controls for the time-constant unobserved individual level. characteristics
      • $\epsilon_{it}$ is an error term assumed to be i.i.d.
For identification strategy, I use matching and two-way fixed effects model.

To address systematic differences between students attending SS schools and coeducational schools, I conducted a matching on observables to ensure that the two groups of students are closely matched in terms of their observed characteristics. and in addition use difference-in-difference methods to eliminate further unobserved heterogeneity that might be present.

Robustness Check: Controling for School Characteristics


so, if the SS schools are characteristically different from co-educational schools, the effects we show in the previous slides may not simply boil down to a peer effect. As a robustness check, I integrate school-level characteristics to account for systematic school-level differences between the schools, this includes number of students, expenditures per student, number of full-time teacher, and student-teacher ratio. As shown in this table, the inclusion of these school characteristics as controls does not appear to change the primary estimates.

There is a possibility that systematic differences in teacher quality, and other unobservable factors related to the autonomy that exist between the two types of schools. Even though teacher sorting doesn’t occur within public schools, as teachers are periodically reassigned to schools within a school district, private schools, which make up 85% of single-sex schools in Seoul, enjoy greater autonomy in their teacher hiring processes (Hahn, Wang and Yang, 2018). If higher-quality teachers tend to opt for private single-sex schools, it is possible that the observed effects in my analysis could be influenced by this systematic disparity in teacher quality. To further ensure the robustness of my findings, I conducted an additional analysis where I excluded private single-sex schools from the dataset and focused solely on public coeducational and single-sex schools. This approach addresses concerns about teacher selection bias. The results of this analysis are presented in Table 6, which consistently demonstrates the positive effect of single-sex schools on girls.
